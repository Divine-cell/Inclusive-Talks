Religion: The Accidental Enemy

written By: Kemuel Kefas

5 min read. June 15 2023

Photo Credit: Freepik

Where I come from, the worst enemy is religion. Religion is simply a set of principles that guides our way of life. Merriam-Webster dictionary defines it as “an organized system of beliefs, ceremonies, and rules used to worship a god or a group of gods”. This singular institution has influenced and shaped not just the beliefs and mindsets of people, but perhaps even the affairs of nations. Religion plays an unfathomable role in the civilization and development of nations, both in the past and present.

Religions achieve their influence either by directly affecting the content of a nation’s constitution and laws, or indirectly by influencing the individuals who are in power. Either way, people are the vehicles used by religion to achieve such influence. Perhaps you must have heard that “everything rises and falls with leadership”. If you do, then we have something in common because I did too. However, the disparity between us might be that I wanted to understand what influences leadership and how it is administered. I discovered that people are products of their mindsets. So are leaders.

Mindset, according to the Merriam-Webster dictionary again, is “a particular way of thinking: a person’s attitude or set of opinions about something”. Imagine a rural settlement. Imagine this settlement having pathways, some leading to the stream, some leading to the marketplace, and some to the king’s palace. Now, imagine that you want to go to the stream. There is a particular path that you would take without having to think it through. Your friend as well, from her house, would use a particular path to get to the stream. You would not change your default path unless there is a need to. The anecdote above is a representation of how the mind works. These pathways are mindsets. We tend to have a particular way of thinking, without proper consideration. It would be interesting to remind you that mindsets are not inborn. They were acquired while growing up.,

Cultures, beliefs, and sentiments that our parents or whoever raised us had, form the basic foundation of these mindsets. These foundations give us a framework to build upon, and most of the time with limited adjustment. A child grows up to be more like his parents, believing in what they believe and most likely practicing the religion of his parents. I need to remind you that these beliefs and religious norms that a child learns do not have to be true, right, or generally good. It just has to be acceptable to the family and at least a few people outside the family. Remember, the exposure or educational status of the parent also is not relevant. This child grows up and is sent into society to represent and portray all that they have learned in their families and schools. We would be fortunate if the child, who becomes a grown-up, was taught to respect everyone, irrespective of their religious or political affiliation.

However, this grown-up becomes a threat to peaceful coexistence when they are taught that two or more religions are not meant to coexist. It becomes a threat to national unity when individuals do not learn that our strength lies in our diversity, not our uniformity. It even becomes worse when such a person occupies a public office because they would have sentimental and favorite tendencies.

Photo Credit: Adobe Stock

Elections into public offices, the appointment of government officials, and even admission into institutions become compromised, as favoritism is shown along religious lines. Merit and competence are being forfeited for religious dominance and over mutual respect among religions.

“Good” and “bad” become synonymous with “my religion” and “their religion” as a result of misinformation that started in childhood. Human beings are perceived not as such, but can be biased as a result of their religious affiliation. This misinformation, misunderstanding, and misinterpretation of religion have led to wars that should not have been fought, losses that could have been avoided, and pains that should never be felt. It costs the government: resources, families: property, communities: harmony, and individuals: their peace of mind.

The Covid-19 pandemic has come and gone. One thing I am glad about is that it teaches us all a lesson. It teaches us a lesson that there is a bigger enemy that does not differentiate between the religious affiliation of its victims. It attacks without mercy, and its sting pierces into the soul of anyone it can get without religious bias. The pandemic has taught us that our strength lies, not in our highlighted differences, but in our abilities to use our unique diversity to devise sustainable means of defeating our bigger enemies. These enemies are not just the pandemic, but also climate change, global health issues, hunger, and poverty, among others. We must learn that religion is not a tool for creating conflicts and trampling over the fundamental human rights of others, but a medium for aligning ourselves with a supernatural being that we have faith in.

We must also, as a matter of urgency and for our good, understand that at some points, religious leaders and their followers have allowed their chauvinistic whims and caprices to cloud their judgment about followers of other religions. This has, of course, led to false and incomplete teachings that have misled many. I believe no religion would choose its dominance over the safety of its faithful. That is why we must desist from every form of violence and sentiment that arises from religious teachings and poses a threat to the peace and harmony that should exist among brothers and sisters.

We must love one another just as we would love ourselves, and we must learn to overcome the temptation of giving preferences to people of the same religion as ourselves when we know that should not be. We must learn rather to teach and encourage that which would assist in qualifying the citizens of our nations, in the affairs which they pursue. We must also learn that we are not safe when our religion is safe, but when our nation is safe because that would give us a wonderful avenue to practice our religion without any form of fear or worry.
